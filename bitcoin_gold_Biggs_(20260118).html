Bitcoin's Gold Price - History, Model, and Falsifiable Predictions through 2035

S. James Biggs

San Diego, California, USA

(January 2026)

Abstract

We present a saturating exponential model that predicts Bitcoin's long-term valuation relative to gold based on their fundamental supply dynamics. Gold's 2% annual supply growth creates a persistent "leak" in its store-of-value function, while Bitcoin's capped supply approaches zero inflation. Our model, fitted to 10 years of data (2015-2024), achieves R² = 0.91 with only three free parameters and correctly predicted 2025 test data within 3%. We document a statistically significant volatility regime change beginning January 2023, with standard deviation falling from σ = 0.478 to σ = 0.258 (p < 0.0001), coinciding with geopolitical shifts in reserve asset preferences following the 2022 weaponization of dollar reserves. The model projects Bitcoin will reach 37 oz gold per Bitcoin by 2030 (95% CI: 22-62 oz) and 43 oz by 2035 (95% CI: 26-72 oz), with expected annual outperformance of 8.6% above gold. These projections represent falsifiable hypotheses about both the trajectory and volatility of Bitcoin’s value relative to Gold.

1.What question are we asking?

Gold and Bitcoin both serve as scarce stores of value. One leaks supply while the other does not.

Can the difference in their abundance explain Bitcoin's long-term value trajectory relative to Gold? And, if so, what does that imply for real returns and global reserve composition?

This inquiry began without a thesis, only data and the curiosity to see what the data would say. The goal was to fit a model consistent with observable prices and simple, defensible assumptions about supply. The theory followed from the fit.

What emerged was a model of the long-term value of Bitcoin. It suggests Bitcoin adoption isn't reaching a saturation point. Rather, it follows a saturation curve. Modeling that curve tells us where it's likely to be headed.

3.What are the saturation rules for each currency?

Gold:

Gold's above-ground stock increases by roughly 2% per year due to mining. This leak is small but persistent, making the total supply effectively infinite over long timescales. Gold's supply curve is elastic to price. If the supply declines and the price rises, mining output increases, reinforcing the equilibrium around 2%.

Bitcoin:

Bitcoin's issuance is strictly limited by protocol. The reward for mining new blocks halves approximately every four years ("the halving cycle"), and the algorithm self-adjusts mining difficulty to maintain a steady block rate of about one block per 10 minutes. The total supply is capped at 21 million Bitcoins, with about 95% already mined as of 2025. The blockchain ensures immutability. No central authority can create new Bitcoins or dilute existing supply.

Accessibility and integrity:

Bitcoin differs from gold not only in scarcity but in distributional fairness. Anyone with an Internet connection can buy or hold Bitcoin directly, without reliance on intermediaries or claims held in custodial accounts. Unlike Gold, where value can be obscured by derivatives or bank-controlled paper claims, Bitcoin's ownership and transactions are transparent and globally verifiable. Moreover, it is increasingly liquid at a consumer level, already exchangeable for goods via networks like PayPal and Square.

4.What model best describes the ratio curve?

Given Gold's 2% leak, and Bitcoin's approach toward zero additional supply, we model the natural logarithm of the Bitcoin’s gold value as a saturating exponential approaching a 2% asymptotic drift:

ln(R(t)) = C + g•t + A(1 - e-λt)

where:

R(t) is Bitcoin’s value in ounces of gold,

t is time in years since the first observation (2015-01),

C is the baseline log ratio,

g is the long-run differential in value growth (fixed to gold's supply rate, g º 0.02),

A and l describe early adoption acceleration and its decay.

Thus, the model has only three free parameters: (C, A, λ). The 2% leak rate (g) is an observed constant, not a tuned variable.

The best-fit parameters seem reasonable (Table 1). The adoption rate of decay (l = 0.283 yr-1) corresponds to a half-life of ln(2)/0.283 ≈ 2.4 years. Thus, the adoption term dampens relatively quickly. It suggests most of the explosive growth has happened in the first ~5-7 years (2015-2022). By the year 2025 (about 4 half-lives), the adoption term is essentially gone, and the continued outperformance of Bitcoin relative to gold is due to its relative scarcity.

4.1 Why This Functional Form?

Alternative approaches to modeling Bitcoin's value include network effect models (Metcalfe's Law: value ∝ users²), stock-to-flow models based on Bitcoin's halving cycle, and regime-switching frameworks. While theoretically interesting, these approaches either:

Require additional parameters (network models need user adoption curves: ≥4 parameters)

Model Bitcoin's absolute USD value rather than its performance relative to gold

Lack the theoretical grounding in observable supply differentials

Our saturating exponential achieves R² = 0.91 with only three free parameters by fixing g to gold's observed supply growth (0.02). This constraint, (anchoring the asymptote to physical reality rather than fitting it), provides both parsimony and interpretability. The functional form directly captures our hypothesis: Bitcoin's early explosive adoption (A, λ terms) eventually saturates, leaving persistent outperformance driven purely by the supply differential (g term).

We prioritize simplicity over complexity. The question isn't "what's the most sophisticated model?" but rather "what's the minimum model sufficient to explain the observed relationship?"

5.How well does the model predict test data?

The fit was made on data through 2024-12, yet it correctly predicts Bitcoin’s gold value for thirteen additional months through the time of this writing, mid-January 2026 (Figure 5, red squares). It is within a few percent of the value observed at the time of this writing (mid-January 2026, ≈ 21 oz Gold per Bitcoin). This forward accuracy demonstrates that the model's behavior isn't overfitted. It appears to capture the underlying dynamic, not coincidence.

All test points cluster near the projected value, well within the 2σ bands (as expected statistically), and (surprisingly), they all lie within the ±1σ bounds, hinting that volatility may be decreasing with time.

6.What does the model predict going forward?

Fitting the model with different assumptions about the additional supply of gold added yearly from mining shows the fit is not sensitive to assumptions about the fixed parameter g. Choices of g = 1.5%, 2.0%, and 2.5% all yield approximately the same story a decade into the future.

Current ratio (mid-January 2026): ≈ 21 oz gold per BTC

Projected (2030-01): ≈ 36-38 oz gold per BTC

Projected (2035-01): ≈ 41-45 oz gold per BTC

The model's insensitivity to the precise value of g (tested from 1.5% to 2.5%) demonstrates that its predictions depend primarily on the existence of gold's supply growth rather than its exact magnitude, making the framework robust to uncertainty in mining economics.

Return:

The model predicts Bitcoin's Compound Annual Growth Rate compared to Gold going forward averages 8.6%/yr (≈ 2.3× per decade). The components of Bitcoin's expected return are:

BTC vs Gold ratio growth: 8.6%/yr, the model's core prediction for how Bitcoin appreciates relative to Gold

Gold real drift: +2.0%/yr, Gold's historical appreciation vs USD (above inflation)

CPI inflation: +2.5%/yr, assumed baseline inflation

Total nominal return: ≈13.1%/yr, Bitcoin's expected USD appreciation

The 8.6% BTC/Gold ratio growth is the key model output; the other components are external assumptions. Bitcoin's projected real return (above inflation) is 10.6%/yr.

Although Bitcoin's expected rate of return compares favorably to Gold, its volatility makes the return at any given moment uncertain. Projecting Bitcoin's historical volatility into the future produces predictions with a wide range:

Projected (2030-01): ≈ 37.2 oz gold per Bitcoin, (23.1 to 59.9 with 68% probability)

Projected (2035-01): ≈ 43.2 oz gold per Bitcoin, (26.8 to 69.6 with 68% probability)

These are wide ranges. A 68% confidence interval from 23 to 60 ounces is as much “guessing” as it is “prediction.” 

Fortunately, another aspect of the data changes the picture entirely: the volatility itself is collapsing. As Bitcoin matures along its adoption curve, the wild price swings attenuate. The market stabilizes. Next we examine this aspect of Bitcoin’s Gold value.

7.How is Bitcoin's volatility changing with time?

To get at this question of volatility, we look at the residuals, the cycles of boom-and-bust superimposed on Bitcoin's upward arc with respect to gold. The analysis shows that the amplitude of these cycles is decreasing with time as Bitcoin becomes established.

A qualitative trend toward declining volatility is evident in the data, with scatter decreasing noticeably after 2023 (Figure 7, left, post-2023 points). To get a more quantitative intuition, the data are transformed to log coordinates (Figure 7, right) so that doubling and halving get equal magnitude, and absolute values of the excursions are plotted so that both decreases and increases of value show as positive volatility. A 36-month rolling average shows the volatility trend with time (Figure 7, right, red line).

Even when the entirety of the data series is fit (Figure 8, left) the trend toward decreased volatility is evident (R² = 0.6419), and statistically significant (p<0.0001). A linear fit of the 36-month rolling average shows a decline of about 0.033 per year. The statistics confirm that the system is stabilizing as adoption progresses.

Volatility declines, but to what value? Obviously, it does not go to zero. The model will never be perfect. To get an approximate value for recent volatility, we divide the data into two parts, pre-2023 and 2023-present (Figure 8, right). A Levene Test compares the variability before and after 2023, and finds the difference to be statistically significant:

Levene Test Results: F(1,131) = 23.18, p < 0.0001 (highly significant)

Pre-2023: σ = 0.478

Post-2023: σ = 0.254 (58% reduction)

Fresh +/-1σ error bounds (Figure 8, right, green dotted lines), are tighter than error bounds based on the training data (Figure 8, right, red dotted lines). Based on post-2023 data, it is reasonable to forecast Bitcoin’s future value in ounces of Gold (initially estimated in Figure 6), but with tighter post-2023 bounds. Before making that forecast, however, it is worth considering the causes of the reduced volatility that we see in the data.

The data show volatility really is stabilizing. The statistics are unambiguous (p < 0.0001). But statistics don't explain causes. A 58% drop in volatility doesn't just happen. Something fundamental shifted in how the world treats Bitcoin. The timing points to a specific event.

8.Causes of reduced volatility - Political Legitimization and Monetary Realignment: 2022-2025

The timing of the volatility regime-change we observe beginning in 2023 suggests a cascade of geopolitical and regulatory shifts that fundamentally altered Bitcoin's institutional status.

The trigger was February 2022: Western governments froze $300 billion in Russian central bank reserves following the Ukraine invasion [11]. This unprecedented weaponization of the dollar-based financial system sent shockwaves through non-aligned nations. Central banks purchased 1,082 tons of gold in 2022, then 1,037 tons in 2023, and 1,045 tons in 2024, making three consecutive years exceeding 1,000 tons for the first time in history [12].

Bitcoin benefited from this same flight to sanctions-resistant assets. The January 2023 volatility breakpoint we identify mathematically appears consistent with lag between geopolitical shock and institutional response.

The 2024 U.S. election accelerated what geopolitics had initiated. The January 2024 approval of spot Bitcoin ETFs opened floodgates [13]. Following the November election, Bitcoin rose from $68,000 to over $100,000 without significant pullbacks. The March 2025 Executive Order establishing a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve formalized what markets had already recognized: Bitcoin had become a legitimate reserve asset.

This political legitimization catalyzed corporate treasury adoption. Over 80 publicly traded companies now hold Bitcoin, up from 58 just two years prior, a 139% increase [14]. Microsoft shareholders may have voted down a Bitcoin allocation, but companies controlling 1.3 million BTC didn't wait for permission [15]. When Trump Media purchased $2 billion in Bitcoin securities and the White House appointed a crypto czar, the reputational risk that kept Fortune 500 CFOs away evaporated.

The volatility reduction we observe mathematically, σ falling from 0.478 to 0.258 at the January 2023 breakpoint, captures this transition.

Critical limitation: This calculation assumes our model parameters are known with certainty. In reality, both μ and σ are estimates from limited data. While the U.S. Strategic Reserve and institutional adoption suggest a regime change, monetary history counsels caution—the Bretton Woods system seemed permanent until 1971, and gold was "demonetized" until central banks reversed course. Bitcoin’s post-2023 "low volatility regime" spans only 2 years and may not persist. When parameter uncertainty is high, the optimal Kelly fraction can be dramatically lower than the naive calculation, which is why practitioners commonly use Half Kelly or Quarter Kelly. Given that even legitimate monetary regime changes can reverse (see: Gold's 1980-2000 bear market), fractional Kelly isn't just conservatism. It is a hedge against the unknown unknows of the future.

Portfolio Allocation: The Reserve Asset Bucket

Bitcoin and Gold both function as reserve assets—pools of value investors hold as insurance against uncertainty. "Black swan" events combine low historical frequency with outsized impact: wars, natural disasters, pandemics, sovereign debt defaults, hyperinflation, technological disruptions, political coups, and financial panics.

Recent history provides abundant examples. The financial collapses of Weimar Germany (1923), the Soviet Union (1991), Argentina (2001), Zimbabwe (2008), Greece (2010-2015), and Venezuela (2016-present) demonstrate how quickly monetary stability can evaporate. At this writing, interest on US federal debt consumes 20% of federal receipts and is projected to reach 30% within ten years. Hyperinflation, outright default, or prolonged stagnation (as Japan experienced from 1991-2010) remain possible responses. The "de-dollarization" trend, which began gradually in the 1990s, has accelerated dramatically since 2022, calling into question the dollar's role as global reserve currency.

Rational investors may reasonably differ in their assessments of black swan probability over the coming decade. Accordingly, they will allocate different proportions to reserve assets. An investor confident that "everything is fine" might hold 3% in reserves. One who views the outlook as "quite uncertain" might reasonably hold 50%.

Whatever the bucket size, a key question remains: Within that reserve bucket, what proportion should be Bitcoin versus Gold?

The Kelly criterion (Kelly 1956) provides a mathematically optimal answer. Given an investment's expected return (μ) and volatility (σ) relative to a baseline, the optimal allocation is:

f* = μ / σ²

Where:

f* = optimal Kelly fraction (proportion to allocate to Bitcoin within the reserve bucket)

μ = 8.58% (Bitcoin's expected outperformance vs Gold)

σ = 0.2544 (post-2023 volatility of BTC/Gold ratio, log space)

Here we treat Gold as the baseline within the reserve bucket. Bitcoin is the growth option relative to that baseline.

The full Kelly allocation yields approximately 100% Bitcoin, 0% Gold (Figure 10, left column). With μ/σ² exceeding 100%, Kelly actually suggests leveraged Bitcoin exposure; within an unleveraged reserve bucket, this means maximum Bitcoin allocation. Conservative investors often use "Half Kelly" (50% Bitcoin) or "Quarter Kelly" (25% Bitcoin), reflecting different risk preferences while remaining data-informed.

The framework separates two independent decisions:

Bucket size reflects the investor's black swan conviction

Bitcoin/Gold split reflects their balance between mathematical optimization (full Kelly) and traditional conservatism (quarter Kelly)

This allows investors with wildly different macro views to apply the same allocation framework within their chosen reserve position.

Important Context: The Kelly criterion assumes perfect model accuracy. In practice:

Model uncertainty means these allocations could prove suboptimal.

If Bitcoin were to significantly underperform gold when “crisis insurance” was most needed, a Bitcoin-heavy reserve would provide less protection than a gold-heavy allocation.

Due to model uncertainty, many practitioners use fractional Kelly allocations - often 25 to 50% of the calculated optimal. The appropriate fraction depends on one’s confidence in how well the model’s parameters hold up in the future.

These calculations optimize within the reserve bucket only, not total portfolio risk.

The key risk is not devastating loss, but rather ineffective insurance - having reserves that insufficiently cushion the blow of a crisis.

10.How does this compare to prior work?

Institutional studies—Deutsche Bank (2025), Fidelity (2024), WisdomTree (2025), NYDIG (2024), and academic studies (Zwick and Syed, 2019)—acknowledge the Bitcoin–Gold analogy but omit the quantitative supply-differential term.

This analysis fills that gap by proposing a reasonable saturating model that links long term supply to valuation drift. Based on this assumption it makes quantitative predictions of future valuations that are consistent with past performance.

Several nations are on the cusp of establishing bitcoin reserves, led by the US with an executive order 2025-03. Basel Committee rules now allow banks to hold up to 2% of reserves in cryptocurrencies starting January 2025.

12.Model Breakers

Future regulation or taxation could alter adoption rates.

Competing digital currencies could displace Bitcoin, decreasing adoption.

Consensus layer or network security risks such as quantum computing could increase the risk of Bitcoin, reducing or reversing adoption.

Transition of Bitcoin to a "consumer exchange" currency could increase its value beyond projections of this “reserve” model.

Changes in Gold mining such as new methods or reserves could increase the annual rate of increase of above-ground Gold.

•         New industrial uses could reduce the availability of Gold for reserve banking.

•         AI-driven displacement of workers could force them to liquidate their personal Bitcoin holdings to pay for food and shelter while unemployed, creating sustained selling pressure and reduced price during an economic transition of unknown duration.

13.Conclusions

From open data and minimal assumptions, Bitcoin's long-term valuation compared to Gold follows a measurable, saturating path. The model captures Bitcoin's transition from speculative investment to established asset.

The price of Bitcoin at the time of this white paper (21 oz gold per bitcoin in mid-January 2026) matches modeled expectations.

Unlike many crypto predictions that claim false precision, we acknowledge the fundamental uncertainty in adoption dynamics and volatility. Our model reflects honest uncertainty while still making a directionally strong claim.

With 95% of Bitcoins mined, the era of 10x moves is ending. The era of sustainable outperformance is beginning.

The rising value of Bitcoin is not a speculative mania. It reflects increasing adoption, decreasing volatility, and superior long-term scarcity compared to Gold. Together these effects signal a slow gravitational re-balancing of monetary mass from metal to bits.

S3. Methods Details

Data Preprocessing: Monthly Bitcoin and Gold prices were collected from public sources. Gold prices were primarily sourced from the World Gold Council's public dataset on GitHub (https://raw.githubusercontent.com/datasets/gold-prices/main/data/monthly.csv) for January 2015 through June 2025. The final seven months (July 2025 - January 2026) were supplemented with data from Yahoo Finance and Kitco (https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/GC=F). Bitcoin prices were obtained from CoinGecko's historical data (https://www.coingecko.com/en/coins/bitcoin/historical_data). Prices were converted to a Bitcoin/gold ratio (ounces of gold per bitcoin) and log-transformed for modeling.

Model Fitting: The saturating exponential model ln(R(t)) = C + g•t + A(1 - e-λt) was fit using nonlinear least squares (Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm) via scipy.optimize.curve_fit. The gold supply growth rate g was fixed at 0.02, leaving three free parameters (C, A, λ).

Volatility Analysis: Residuals were split at January 2023. A Levene test compared pre- and post-2023 variances (p < 0.0001). Rolling 36-month standard deviations quantified the volatility trend (-0.033/year decline rate).

Projections: Future values were projected using fitted parameters. Confidence intervals used the post-2023 volatility (σ = 0.258) for ±1σ and ±2σ bounds. Sensitivity analysis tested g = 1.5%, 2.0%, and 2.5% supply growth rates.
